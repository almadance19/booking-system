Core Teaching Principles

This section covers the foundational concepts of effective teaching and progressive learning. These principles form the bedrock of a well-structured class, ensuring students not only learn the steps but truly understand the movement.

Pillars of Good Teaching

Clarity

Break movements into logical, digestible steps.

Progression

Always move from simple to complex, building on prior knowledge.

Adaptation

Adjust explanations for beginners vs. advanced dancers.

Repetition + Variation

Repeat to build muscle memory, but vary to keep interest.

Feedback Loop

Observe students, correct gently, and encourage self-discovery.

Layering the Learning

Level 1: Mechanics

What are my feet doing? Focus on the basic steps and patterns.

Level 2: Technique

How is my body supporting balance, connection, and isolation?

Level 3: Musicality

Where does this movement fit within the rhythm and melody of the music?

Level 4: Expression/Style

How can I make this movement my own and express my personality?

Teaching Inspiration from Other Sources

Great teachers are also great students. This section explores how to effectively and ethically incorporate material learned from other classes, workshops, or online videos into your own teaching practice.

From Other Classes

  • Adapt, don't just copy. Make the material fit your unique style and your students' specific needs.
  • Credit the source when relevant. This is especially important in teacher development settings to maintain professional integrity.

From Videos / Online

  • Analyze carefully. Deconstruct the timing, lead/follow mechanics, and musical application before you attempt to teach it.
  • Simplify. Online videos often show advanced, flashy variations. Your job is to create a beginner-friendly entry point.
  • Build progressions. Introduce the foundational basics first, then layer the "inspired" technique on top.

The Teacher's Toolbox

Use Keywords

Develop short cues instead of long explanations.

Be Visual + Verbal

Demonstrate and describe the movement simultaneously.

Vary Formats

Switch between individual, partner, and group exercises.

Encourage Reflection

Ask students, “How did that feel for you?”

Apply Progressive Overload

Each week, add one new challenge on top of known material to ensure continuous growth.

Meta-Teaching Exercise

Put your knowledge to the test. This exercise is designed for you and your fellow teachers to practice the principles discussed today, reinforcing the framework through hands-on application and peer feedback.

  1. Task: Pick a move you learned recently from a workshop or video.
  2. Prepare: Break it down using the Movement Breakdown Framework discussed in this guide.
  3. Teach: Instruct a small group on the move in a concise 5–10 minute session.
  4. Feedback: Receive constructive peer feedback on your clarity, progression, and adaptation to the group's needs.
